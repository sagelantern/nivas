# Stay Nivas — Editorial Guide

## The Stay Nivas Review

Every review answers one question: **What is it like to be here?**

Not "what does this hotel have" but "what does this hotel *do* to you." The amenity list is on the property's website. We exist to tell the story the property can't tell about itself.

---

## Review Structure

Reviews follow a loose arc that mirrors an actual stay. Not rigid sections with headers — more like movements in a piece of music. The reader should feel like they're checking in alongside you.

### 1. Arrival (The Threshold)

The first impression. Not "we arrived at 3pm and checked in." More: what shifted when you crossed from the outside world into this place? What did you notice first — a scent, a sound, a material, the way someone greeted you?

This section sets the tone. It should make the reader feel something within the first three sentences.

### 2. The Space (Dwelling)

The room, the grounds, the public spaces. But described through *experience*, not inventory.

**Instead of:** "The room featured a king bed, a rain shower, and a private balcony with ocean views."

**Write:** "The bed faced the window — no TV competing for your attention, just the water. Whoever designed this room decided what you should wake up to, and they were right."

Key questions to address:
- What choices did the designers make, and what do those choices say about how they want you to feel?
- What surprised you?
- What would you change?

### 3. Nourishment (Food & Drink)

Food as experience, not as a Yelp review. What does the food program tell you about the property's identity? Is it performative or genuine? Does the breakfast feel like an afterthought or a philosophy?

Specific dishes are worth naming when they reveal something. A generic buffet description is not.

### 4. The People (Hosting)

How the staff made you feel. This is where *atithi devo bhava* lives — the Indian lens of sacred hosting. Not "service was excellent" but specific moments that revealed whether this property genuinely cares about its guests or is going through motions.

The small things: Did they learn your preferences? Did they anticipate needs without being intrusive? Did they create space for you or fill it?

### 5. Stillness (The Intangible)

The thing that's hardest to review and matters most. The quieter observations. How the property relates to its surroundings — does it honor the place or ignore it? Was there a moment of genuine peace or beauty? Something you'd tell a close friend about?

This is the section that makes Stay Nivas different. Most reviewers skip it because it's hard to write. We don't skip it.

### 6. Departure (Would You Return?)

Not a star rating. Not a pros/cons list. Simply: did this place earn a return? And for whom — couples, families, solo travelers seeking quiet?

One sentence on who this property is *really* for, even if that's different from who they market to.

## Writing Principles

### Lead with Sensory Detail

The reader should see, hear, smell, and feel the property through your writing. Abstract praise ("beautiful," "luxurious," "world-class") is banned. Replace every adjective with a specific observation.

**No:** "The spa was incredible."
**Yes:** "The treatment room smelled like lemongrass and was dead silent except for water moving somewhere beneath the floor."

### Be Honest, Not Harsh

If something didn't work, say so — but with the specificity and care of someone who understands how hard hospitality is. We are not takedown artists. We are guests who notice everything and speak with respect.

**No:** "The breakfast was disappointing."
**Yes:** "Breakfast leaned on a buffet that felt designed for volume, not for this property. The made-to-order dosa station was the exception — clearly someone in the kitchen cared about getting that right."

### The Indian Lens (Light Touch)

Don't force it. But when a natural connection exists between what you're experiencing and your cultural background, name it. An Ayurvedic spa treatment at an Alila. The way a Japanese ryokan's hosting philosophy mirrors your grandmother's. The particular way chai is served — or butchered.

This is what makes Stay Nivas a point of view, not just another review site.

### Family When Relevant

When traveling with family, mention it naturally. How a property handled a toddler. Whether the vibe shifted with kids present. But this is not a family travel blog — the family appears when they're part of the story, not as the story.

### Length & Pacing

- Target: 1,200–2,000 words per review.
- Shorter is fine for city hotels or shorter stays.
- No padding. If a section has nothing interesting to say, skip it.
- Use line breaks generously. The reading experience should feel spacious.

## What We Don't Do

- **Star ratings or numerical scores.** A property is not a number.
- **Affiliate links or sponsored content.** The moment a GM suspects you're pay-to-play, the relationship is worthless.
- **Listicles.** No "Top 10 Hotels in Bali." We review one property at a time, with depth.
- **Points/miles talk.** Redemption strategies are for other sites.
- **Complaints about price.** Our audience knows what things cost. We evaluate value through experience, not line items.
- **Photography without purpose.** No bathroom mirror selfies, no "look at my plate" shots unless the plating genuinely tells a story.

---

## Headline Style

Property name first, then a phrase that captures the essence — not a clickbait hook.

**Examples:**
- *Alila Villas Uluwatu — Hospitality as Architecture*
- *The Beekman — A Lobby That Remembers New York*
- *Andaz Delhi — When a Chain Hotel Forgets It's a Chain*
- *Soho House Barcelona — The Pool You Earn by Not Trying*

---

## Metadata Per Review

Each review includes a quiet metadata block (not the focus, but useful for readers and GMs):

```
Property: [Name]
Location: [City, Country]
Dates: [Month Year]
Room: [Room type]
Traveled: [Solo / Couple / Family]
Nights: [Number]
Would Return: [Yes / Not Yet / No]
```

"Not Yet" is for properties that have potential but aren't there. It's more useful to a GM than "No" — it says "I'm watching."
